A photography workshop inside a living sacred tattoo tradition

Walk around Thailand long enough and you start noticing the tattoos. The old man selling dried fish at the market. The guy in front of you at the 7-Eleven with the points of a geometric tattoo showing above the back of his t-shirt. Once you know what you're looking at, you see it everywhere. This workshop puts you inside the ceremony where it's still being made.

These are magic spells cast into your skin. The designs are meant to protect you, bring money, bring luck, make you attractive to other people, charming in speech, well-regarded by those around you. Some are meant to make you feared. Some are meant to make you loved. The people applying them have spent years learning the script, the geometry, and the chants that activate each one. And the tattoos themselves, in the hands of a skilled Ajarn, can be genuinely beautiful.

The tradition

Where this comes from

The exact origins of Sak Yant are not fully agreed upon. Historians generally place the practice around the third century, when yantra geometry, used in Hindu and tantric practice across South Asia, arrived in what is now Cambodia, Thailand, and Myanmar. The tradition draws from Hindu geometry, Buddhist Pali scripture, and older animist practice. The tattooing of these designs onto the body, rather than wearing or displaying them, is specific to mainland Southeast Asia.

A note on pronunciation: Thai people say it closer to "suk-yun." The t at the end of Yant is silent. It appears in the romanization because it exists in the Thai spelling.

For most of its history in Thailand, Sak Yant still carried a social stigma. It was associated with criminals, fighters, and people in dangerous occupations. Conservative Thai society largely avoided it.

That began to change in 2003 when Ajarn Noo Kanpai tattooed Angelina Jolie. The global attention that followed introduced the tradition to a western audience and, just as importantly, began removing the stigma inside Thailand itself. In the years since, tattoo culture has expanded globally to a degree that would have been difficult to predict at the time. The market that grew from all of this produced a generation of practitioners oriented toward that audience. The Ajarn at the center of this photography workshop works from that older lineage. His designs predate that market.

The designs this Ajarn works with are older than the ones most people come to Thailand looking for.

On the day

What to expect

    1.

    Van pickup from a central Bangkok meeting point. Early start. The day is long and the space has no air conditioning. Seating is on the floor. This needs to be understood before you commit.

    2.

    A briefing before the session covers how to behave in the space, what to wear, and what to expect from the ceremony. Consent for photography has been established with the Ajarn and communicated to the people being tattooed.

    3.

    You photograph freely during the tattooing session. No posed shots, no directed moments. You work the room the way you would on a documentary assignment.

    4.

    The blessing ceremony takes place after the tattooing. It is a distinct photographic event from the tattooing itself.

    5.

    No social media video. Still photography only. The exact location and the Ajarn's identity are not for public posting. The Bang Bon area of Bangkok is as specific as it needs to be.
